How do you manage multiple implementations happening simultaneously?

"Great question. At Deel, I typically manage 6-8 implementations at various stages. Here's my approach:

1. Structured Project Tracking:
I maintain a master tracker with all projects showing: client name, country, current phase, next milestone, risks, and action items. I update this daily so I always know what's critical. I use color coding—green for on track, yellow for attention needed, red for blockers.

2. Time Blocking:
Monday mornings are for project planning and high-priority escalations. Tuesday-Thursday, I block specific hours for each client—for example, 10-11 AM for Client A's UAT, 2-3 PM for Client B's data validation. Fridays are for documentation, team meetings, and catching up on delayed items.

3. Batching Similar Tasks:
If I have 3 clients in Singapore, I batch their CPF configurations together because the logic is the same. This makes me more efficient. Similarly, I schedule UAT sessions back-to-back so I'm in "testing mode" rather than context-switching constantly.

4. Clear Communication Cadence:
Every client gets a weekly status email and a fixed meeting slot. I set expectations upfront: "We'll meet Wednesdays at 3 PM, and I'll send updates every Friday." This reduces ad-hoc calls and keeps everyone aligned.

5. Risk Flags:
I proactively flag risks before they become issues. If a client is delayed in sending data, I don't wait—I send a reminder with impact: "If we don't receive master data by Friday, our go-live will shift from Nov 15 to Nov 29."

6. Team Collaboration:
For complex items, I delegate. If I need custom reports built, I loop in our analytics team early. If there's a product limitation, I escalate to engineering immediately rather than waiting.

Example:
Last quarter, I had 7 projects: Hong Kong MPF setup, Singapore cutover, Indonesia parallel run, UAE gratuity configuration, and 3 Egypt transitions. I kept them organized by using Monday mornings for priority setting, maintained my tracker religiously, and held daily 15-minute standups with my team to surface blockers. All 7 went live on time with zero payroll errors.

The key is staying organized, communicating proactively, and knowing when to ask for help."

How do you ensure implementations stay on schedule when clients are slow to respond or provide information?

"Client delays are one of the biggest risks in implementations, so I'm very proactive about managing this.

1. Set Expectations Upfront:
During kickoff, I clearly outline what I need from the client and by when. I explain dependencies: "If we don't receive employee data by Week 3, our UAT will shift to Week 5, which delays go-live." This helps them understand their role is critical, not passive.

2. Make It Easy for Them:
I provide pre-built templates with clear instructions and examples. Instead of saying "send me employee data," I send an Excel sheet with column headers, sample rows, and data validation rules. This reduces back-and-forth because they know exactly what format I need.

3. Proactive Reminders:
I don't wait until the deadline to follow up. If data is due Friday, I check in on Wednesday: "Just confirming you're on track to send master data by Friday. Let me know if you need help or have questions." This gives them time to course-correct.

4. Escalation with Empathy:
If a client is consistently delayed, I escalate respectfully. I'll say: "I completely understand you're juggling multiple priorities. However, our go-live date is at risk. Can we schedule 30 minutes this week so I can walk you through the data template together? That might make it faster."

5. Build in Buffer:
I never plan a project with zero buffer. If I think UAT needs 1 week, I allocate 1.5 weeks. This gives me flexibility if the client is delayed without impacting overall timelines.

6. Document Everything:
I keep an email trail of all requests and send weekly status updates highlighting outstanding items. If delays become chronic, I have documentation to show the client where we lost time—this helps if timelines need formal adjustment.

Example:
I had a Singapore client who was 2 weeks late sending employee data because their HR manager was on leave. Instead of just waiting, I contacted their finance team and offered to help extract data directly from their old system. They appreciated the proactive approach, we recovered 1 week, and only missed final deadline by 3 days instead of 2 weeks.

Bottom Line:
I balance empathy with accountability. I understand clients are busy, but I also make sure delays don't snowball into bigger problems by staying visible, helpful, and solutions-oriented."
